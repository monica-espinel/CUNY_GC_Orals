José Guadalupe Posada

José Guadalupe Posada (1852–1913) was a pioneering Mexican printmaker, illustrator, and cartoonist famed for his satirical depictions of skeletons ("calaveras"), which became essential imagery for the Mexican Day of the Dead celebrations. His iconic La Calavera Catrina, later popularized by Diego Rivera, remains one of Mexico's most recognizable cultural symbols.

Aguascalientes, Mexico 1852 (birth); early training 1852–1872 Birthplace. Early artistic talent refined at Academy of Fine Arts. Apprenticed at printing house of José Trinidad Pedroza learning lithography and engraving. Early satirical cartoons for local newspapers
León, Guanajuato, Mexico 1872–1888 Commercial lithography, teaching. Survived devastating León flood which he depicted in print. Flood documentation prints
Mexico City, Mexico 1889–1913 Flourishing production and collaboration with publisher Antonio Vanegas Arroyo. Mass dissemination of images on broadsides, books, songbooks, newspapers. Created thousands of calavera images. La Calavera Catrina, Calavera Oaxaqueña, Gran Calavera Eléctrica
