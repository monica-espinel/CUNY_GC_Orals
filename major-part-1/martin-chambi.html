Martín Chambi

Martín Chambi (1891–1973) is celebrated as one of Latin America's greatest photographers and the first major Indigenous Latin American photographer. His profound documentation of Andean culture, especially through his dignified portraits of indigenous peoples and photographs of Machu Picchu, helped redefine Peruvian national identity and the representation of Indigenous communities.

Places Lived and Stylistic Influence

Place Period Stylistic Relationship Key Artworks
Coaza, Puno, Peru 1891 (birth) Birthplace in Quechua-speaking family. First encountered photography through English engineers at gold mine where father worked. Early inspiration
Arequipa, Peru 1908–1917 Moved to apprentice with renowned photographer Max T. Vargas. Mastered lighting, composition, and portraiture. Established first studio in Sicuani (1917). Training period works
Cusco, Peru 1920–1973 Opened studio in 1920. Achieved greatest fame here. Became both photojournalist recording festivals and events, and leading portrait photographer of high society. Portrayed Indigenous compatriots with dignity and humanity, challenging colonial stereotypes. Machu Picchu photographs, portraits of campesinos, artisans, Varayoc (Indigenous mayors)
