Wifredo Lam

Wifredo Lam (1902–1982) was a pivotal Cuban artist whose work merged Surrealism with Afro-Cuban cultural and spiritual influences. Born to a Chinese father and a mother of African, Spanish, and indigenous descent, his multicultural background profoundly shaped his artistic vision. His masterpiece The Jungle (1943) stands as one of the most important works of 20th-century Latin American art.

Sagua La Grande, Cuba 1902 (birth) Birthplace. Early exposure to Afro-Cuban rites and Santería through family and godmother. Multicultural heritage shaped artistic vision. Early influences
Madrid, Spain 1923–1938 Studied European modernist movements. Absorbed influences from Bosch, Goya, Bruegel. Fought in Spanish Civil War. Early European-influenced works
Paris, France 1938–1941; later periods Met Picasso who became mentor and friend. Connected with Surrealists including André Breton, Miró, Matisse, Léger. Embraced automatism and surrealist techniques. Surrealist-influenced works
Havana, Cuba 1941–late 1950s Returned during WWII. Synthesized Cuban and African visual traditions with European Modernism. Created masterworks exploring Afro-Cuban spirituality. The Jungle (1943), Zambezia, Zambezia (1950)
