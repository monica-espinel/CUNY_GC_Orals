Places Lived and Stylistic Influence

Place Period Stylistic Relationship Key Artworks
Berlin, Germany 1899–1922 Born Annelise Elsa Frieda Fleischmann to an affluent Berlin family. Early artistic interests were encouraged; studied art before enrolling at Bauhaus. Early student works
Weimar/Dessau, Germany (Bauhaus) 1922–1933 Entered Bauhaus in 1922. Directed to the weaving workshop due to gender restrictions, but transformed it into her primary artistic medium. Studied under Gunta Stölzl. Married Josef Albers in 1925. Became head of the weaving workshop in 1931. Her diploma piece—a sound-absorbing, light-reflecting wallcovering—was a major innovation. Wall covering for auditorium (1929), Design for a Wall Hanging (1927)
Black Mountain, North Carolina 1933–1949 Emigrated to the US after Nazi closure of Bauhaus. Josef led the art program at Black Mountain College while Anni established and led the weaving program. Developed curriculum emphasizing material experimentation. Influenced students including Ruth Asawa. Weavings and textile experiments from Black Mountain period
Latin America (travels) 1930s–1960s Multiple trips to Mexico, Peru, and other Latin American countries profoundly influenced her work. Studied ancient Andean weaving techniques and Pre-Columbian textiles at archaeological sites and museums. Works influenced by Pre-Columbian patterns and techniques
New Haven, Connecticut 1950–1994 Moved when Josef joined Yale University. Continued weaving and began printmaking in the 1960s. Published influential text "On Weaving" (1965). Continued artistic practice until her death. Black-White-Gold I (1950), prints from 1960s–1980s
